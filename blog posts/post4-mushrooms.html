Mushroom - Everything you should know about!


History and Medicinal properties:


Mushrooms, the fruiting bodies of fungi that grow above the ground, have been a part of the human diet and used as both food and medicine for centuries; they are considered as one of the most ancient foods of all time, ancient Greeks believed that mushrooms provided strength for warriors in battle, and the Romans perceived them as the “Food of the Gods”.
Mushroom is one of the best diets, which contains protein, minerals and vitamins like Nutritive material in a sufficient quantity. Mushroom is having less quantity of fats due to this reason it is a very good diet for heart patients and due to the very fewer carbohydrates mushroom is the most suitable diet for diabetic patients.
Mushrooms are popular because of the valuable important nutrients, including selenium, potassium, riboflavin, niacin, vitamin D, proteins, and fiber in them; also they are cholesterol-free and low in calories, carbohydrates, fat and sodium.
Mushrooms are also important for their healing capacities and properties in traditional medicine and act as antibacterial, immune system enhancer and cholesterol-lowering agents. It has beneficial effects on the health and treatment of some diseases. They are also known to lower the risks of cancer.


Role in Modern Culinary:



Mushrooms are fungi, biologically distinct from the plant and animal-derived foods, they have been considered as ingredient of gourmet cuisine across the globe, they are considered as a delicacy with high nutritional and functional value, and they are also accepted as nutraceutical foods.
Mushrooms are classified into food grouping systems by their use as a vegetable, mushroom's increasing use in main dishes in plant-based diets is growing, helping consumers to follow dietary guidance recommendations and especially because of their unique flavour they have been valued by humankind as a culinary wonder.
Mushrooms have many flavors and nutrient characteristics that make them an ideal addition to many dishes. Their texture and savory flavour properties make them a suitable substitute for meat. Mushrooms contribute moisture that improves the mouthfeel and overall sensory appeal of many dishes, they contain about 92% water in them which makes them low-calorie dense and can reduce the energy density of the final dish.
Mushrooms can be used as a substitute for ground beef in dishes such as ground beef-based burger patties because of their meaty flavour and texture although they lack in protein, a blend of ground meat and mushroom can result in a low-calorie dish with that same deliciousness of the meat.
There are more than 2,000 species of mushrooms exist in nature, but around 25 are widely accepted as food and few are commercially cultivated.


Five of the most cultivated mushrooms worldwide :


  1. Agaricus bisporus


    2. These are also known as the white button mushroom,crimini, and portabella; these are the most commonly consumed mushroom in the world and are native to grasslands in Europe and North America. It has two colour states while immature – white and brown.


  2. Lentinus edodes (Shiitake Mushrooms)


    3. It is an edible mushroom native to East Asia, which is cultivated and consumed in many Asian countries. It is considered a medicinal mushroom in some forms of traditional medicine. Shiitake grow in groups on the decaying wood of deciduous trees.


  3. Pleurotus ostreatus (Oyster)


    4. It is another common edible mushroom. It was first cultivated in Germany during World War I and is now grown commercially around the world for food. It is related to the similarly cultivated king oyster mushroom. The oyster mushroom is one of the more commonly sought wild mushrooms.


  4. Volvariella volvacea (Straw)


    5. It is a species of edible mushroom cultivated throughout East and Southeast Asia and used extensively in Asian cuisines. They are often available fresh in Asia but are more frequently found in canned or dried forms outside their nations of cultivation.
    Worldwide, straw mushrooms rank third in consumption, although their use in the West is somewhat uncommon and usually confined to use in Oriental cooking.


  5. Flammulina ostreatus (Enoki)


    6. It is well-known for its role in Japanese cuisine, where it is also known as Enokitake. Commercially farmed enoki is a long, thin white mushroom and is a popular ingredient for soups, especially in East Asian cuisine, but can be used for salads and other dishes. The mushroom has a crisp texture and can be refrigerated for approximately one week.


Mushroom production is continuously increasing, China being the biggest producer around the world. However, wild mushrooms are becoming more important for their nutritional, sensory, and especially pharmacological characteristics, which encourage more people to hunt for these wild mushrooms but hunting wild mushrooms is a tricky task as there are so many poisonous mushrooms which share a similar look with their safer counterparts.
